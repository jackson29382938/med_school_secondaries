Personal Essay for the Medical School Application
Explain your motivation to seek a career in MEDICINE. Be sure to include the value of your experiences that
prepare you to be a physician. *
The first patient I consented for tissue donation laughed through most of our conversation. She was scheduled for an aneurysm clipping
the next morning and joked about her grandkids while she signed. I read the room as calm and left thinking I had handled it well. Two
days later, her nurse told me she had been terrified and had not heard most of what I said.
That night I sat with it. I had treated her composure as agreement. I had explained the protocol clearly and gotten a signature in the right
places. What I had not done was ask what she was afraid of, and because I did not ask, she did not tell me. After that, I stopped treating
composure as understanding, and I started watching for the small signs that someone was making a decision they could not yet say out
loud.
A few weeks later, I sat with a sixty-one-year-old retired math teacher with an unruptured aneurysm. She turned the form over and asked,
"If something goes wrong tomorrow, will the tissue at least help somebody else?" I had a research answer ready, but I did not yet know
how to answer fear. So I slowed down. I told her what we knew and what we did not, and that her decision would not change her care.
She signed, but more importantly she stayed in the room with the choice she had made. I could describe the science, but I was beginning
to understand the responsibility of being the person answering.
Those conversations reshaped how I walked into every other room. At ARMR Systems, while developing wearable hemorrhage-control
devices, I watched physicians ask whether a medic could use the device with shaking hands, blood on the gloves, and minutes left. The
right question was never only whether the seal held. It was whether the person using it could trust it at 3 a.m. with a casualty in front of
them, and whether the casualty's family would later understand what had been decided in those minutes.
That lesson landed hardest during a Walter Reed rotation. A surgeon explained limb salvage to a sergeant whose question was not about
range of motion. He asked whether he would still be able to carry his daughter. The surgeon answered that question before discussing
fixation, flaps, or rehabilitation, and I watched the sergeant exhale for the first time since we had walked in. I went back to the consent
conversations at Barrow that week and started asking patients what they were actually trying to protect. Almost none of them led with the
medical answer.
My Texas connection is where that question became tied to a population. Summers at my Aunt Wendy's ranch in San Antonio led to
mornings at the South Texas VA and afternoons in her glaucoma practice. One veteran did not begin with his eye symptoms; he began
with the drive, the missed work, and whether another procedure would make him dependent on his daughter. My aunt did not rush to the
slit lamp. She earned enough trust for him to say what check-in missed. Across whole afternoons of Vietnam-era and post-9/11 patients, I
came to understand Texas veteran care less as an exposure and more as a community I intend to return to with purpose.
Tanzania taught the other side of the same lesson. At Kilimanjaro Christian Medical Center, I documented cornea-transplant patients with
Lusekelo, our translator and a former recipient now in university. A four-year-old and her father had ridden four days by bus from Mwanza
for one of two corneas drawn from a waitlist in the thousands. An older woman lost her place so the child could go first. I heard her from
the corridor as the child was wheeled in. That morning I learned what care restores to a life and what scarcity takes from another, and that
the people closest to the decision often understand it more honestly than the people making it.
I am drawn toward orthopedic trauma, including the possibility of serving on a Forward Resuscitative Surgical Detachment, because that
is where preparation, anatomy, equipment, and time pressure meet: a small team working forward, with limited resources, to stabilize
patients whose next hours may decide whether they keep a limb, a role, or a life that still feels like their own. The technical work draws
me. What keeps me is what happens after the device works: what the patient understood, what the family missed, and what the system
can still repair. I want medical school to teach me the judgment, humility, and skill to take responsibility for the whole problem: the biology,
the tool, the team, the evacuation chain, and the person trying to return to a life that still feels like their own.

Personal Characteristics
Describe your personal qualities, characteristics, skills, or strengths, and how they will contribute meaningfully to
the lives of others.
A patient missing visual-field prompts rarely gives immediate feedback. A geometry mismatch in a silicone seal does. I have learned to
bring mechanical skepticism into human settings without mistaking patients for machines.
At ARMR Systems, the hemorrhage-control prototype kept failing at the same silicone cover. The easy explanation was manufacturing
variation. I pulled the cover off, ran my finger along the seal, and found a geometry mismatch where the cover seated against the housing.
A brake-component-inspired gasket held overnight. That habit follows me into murine cryosectioning, Python accessibility pipelines, and
CAD work. Its value in medicine is not that I like problems; it is that I am willing to distrust the first explanation when it does not fit the
person or the data.
I also bring steadiness when my preferred role disappears. In rugby, injuries moved me from the field to the sideline, where I wrote
training plans, adjusted lifts for teammates, organized alumni outreach, and stayed accountable to a team I could no longer help by
playing. In ROTC, NEST Strategies, and design teams, I have had to do the same: serve the task instead of protecting the role I wanted.
That habit helps me contribute without needing to be central.
I also bring a habit of reframing what a system is designed to do versus what people actually need from it. On a pediatric chest-tube
design team, one parent shifted our thinking by describing the device less as a tube-retention problem and more as a daily-handling
problem: sleep, bathing, lifting a child, and the fear of causing pain by moving wrong. That interview changed how I understand the idea of
a "secure design." It had to hold mechanically, but it also had to make sense to a caregiver at 2 a.m.
In medical school, I would bring the role I have tried to play on teams before: noticing what is failing, making the hard problem clearer, and
helping the group move. I have led account analysts through client work, cadets through field tasks, and design teams through physician
feedback that contradicted our assumptions. On the abscess-drainage catheter team, the most important thing I did was revise the model
when physician feedback showed that my simulation had optimized the wrong problem. I hope to impart those habits to patients,
classmates, and colleagues: not by needing to have the answer first, but by recognizing when the first answer does not fit the person, the
data, or the need in front of us.

Optional Essay - Unique Experience
Briefly discuss any circumstances or experiences that are relevant to your application which have not previously
been presented.
My freshman fall is the lowest semester on my transcript, and I want to explain it directly. A shoulder surgery I had hoped to avoid, my
father's job loss two months into the year, and a BME program asking for a kind of work I had not yet learned how to do all converged that
fall. I had finished high school confident I knew how to study. The first set of problem sets corrected that quickly.
Every semester since has moved up. The Dean's List terms that followed are not a story about getting smarter; they are a story about
asking the right questions of the right people earlier. I started attending office hours before I needed rescue, found peers who would tell
me when my reasoning was off, and stopped treating not-yet-knowing as something to hide.
That semester also forced me to do something I had been avoiding. I asked my lab PI for a deferred deadline, told my ROTC cadre I
needed modified PT during recovery, and called my dad more than I had before. None of that came easily. Until then, I had treated
endurance as the ability to keep moving without asking for much. I have come to understand asking for help before failure becomes
visible as a discipline rather than a weakness.
A seemingly less significant moment that fall taught me a related habit. During a lecture, a friend's cochlear implant processor began
producing sharp feedback loud enough to interrupt the room. We found grime on the contacts, cleaned and reseated the piece, and the
sound resolved itself. The repair was not the part that stayed with me; it was how quickly a small technical failure became
embarrassment, interruption, and exclusion. The same dynamic appears in the clinic, where a mistranslated discharge instruction or a
missed prompt on a visual-field test can become an outcome much later. It is one reason I have spent so much time on captioning
research and consent conversations.
My transcript shows the cost of learning the first habit. What came after is the part I am most proud of.
